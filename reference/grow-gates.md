# The Grow Gates

The points where a growth loop has to stop and wait for a human. The pipeline runs
the drafting, the variants, the scheduling, the number-pulling. The gates are where
you decide. This is the operating reference — the terse, runnable form. The
narrative version, with a worked example carried through one real product, lives in
the grow field guide.

Each gate names its **lens** — Design, Content, Positioning, or the cross-cutting
Experience lens — so you know when you're on home ground and when to slow down. You
don't need all the grow lenses to use this. Each gate tells you how to work it even
when its lens isn't yours yet.

Unlike the build side, the grow gates don't run in one straight pass. Growth is
three rhythms, and a gate belongs to the rhythm it runs on:

- **Strategy** — set periodically, reused across many pieces.
- **Per-piece** — run on every artifact you publish.
- **Feedback** — continuous, and it closes the loop back to the build side.

**Run Gate ∞ first, every time.** It decides how much a given piece or campaign
earns.

---

## Gate ∞ — How much does this piece deserve?

_Lens: all of them, above the others. The master gate._

- **Decide:** Right-size the effort to the stakes. A reply in a thread earns a glance
  at voice and a send. A launch page earns audience, claim, design, copy, and a
  scheduled rollout.
- **Good judgment:** Read reach and permanence first, and let them set the process.
  Ephemeral and low-reach gets a light touch. Permanent, high-reach, or hard to walk
  back gets the full set. Never confuse "I posted a lot" with "I said the right thing
  in the right place."
- **Not your lens:** The further something reaches, the longer it lives, or the
  harder it is to retract, the more gates it earns. When unsure, treat it as
  higher-reach than it looks — a bad message in front of the right people is the
  expensive mistake.
- **Failure modes:** Full campaign treatment on every throwaway until you burn out.
  Throwaway treatment on the one launch that mattered. Deciding by mood.

## Strategy cadence — set periodically, reused

### Gate 1 — Who's the audience, and what do they need to hear?

_Lens: Positioning._

- **Decide:** Name the specific audience and the one thing they need to hear to act.
  Every per-piece gate builds against this.
- **Good judgment:** The audience is a person in a situation, not a demographic. Map
  what holds them where they are with the four switching forces (push, pull, habit,
  anxiety). Write to the force that's actually binding — usually habit or anxiety —
  not the one that's easiest to write, usually pull.
- **Not your lens:** Have the agent map your audience with the four forces in their
  words, then pick the one force your product genuinely relieves and write to it. If
  you've never done positioning, quote a real customer's reason for switching and aim
  at that.
- **Failure modes:** "Everyone who needs X." Writing to the pull when the customer is
  stuck on anxiety. Setting the audience once and never revisiting it.

### Gate 6-define — What would "it worked" even mean?

_Lens: Positioning, with a Marketing edge._

- **Decide:** Name the observable outcome that counts as success, before you make the
  thing, so the feedback cadence has something real to read.
- **Good judgment:** Pick an outcome metric over an activation metric, and one you'd
  bet money on. Know the difference between "did numbers" and "produced a customer
  conversation you can point to." Define it before you publish so you can't
  rationalize a win after.
- **Not your lens:** Ask "if this works, what changes that I could observe next
  week?" If the answer is only "more impressions," push until it's a behavior — a
  reply, a booked call, a switch. Write it down before you post.
- **Failure modes:** Measuring what's easy instead of what matters. Redefining
  success after the fact. No outcome metric at all.

## Per-piece cadence — every artifact you publish

### Gate 0 — Ready to show?

_Lens: Experience (cross-cutting)._

- **Decide:** Is the destination worth the attention you're about to drive to it? A
  great post pointing at a rough landing page or a broken funnel wastes a first
  impression, which doesn't come back.
- **Good judgment:** Walk the whole path a new person walks, from the post to the
  payoff, and fix the breaks before you drive traffic. Rather delay the promotion
  than burn a warm audience on an experience that isn't ready. Readiness is relative
  to who's arriving and what you led them to expect.
- **Not your lens:** Click your own funnel as if you'd never seen it — where do you
  get confused, stuck, or let down? Have the agent walk the path and list every dead
  end and bounce point. Fix those before you promote.
- **Failure modes:** Driving traffic to a broken funnel. Burning a launch audience on
  a half-ready product. Confusing "the campaign is ready" with "the thing it points
  at is ready."

### Gate 2 — What's the one claim?

_Lens: Positioning, with a Content edge._

- **Decide:** Pick the single claim this piece makes, fit to the Gate 1 audience, and
  cut the rest. One piece, one point.
- **Good judgment:** Say the one true thing that speaks to the binding switching
  force, not the longest feature list. A piece that makes five claims makes none.
- **Not your lens:** Ask the agent for the five things you could say, rank them
  against the Gate 1 audience, keep only the top one. If two feel equal, keep the one
  a skeptic finds hardest to dismiss.
- **Failure modes:** Cramming every benefit into one piece. Leading with the claim
  that's easiest to write instead of the one that moves the customer. Letting the
  agent's full list survive because trimming feels like losing.

### Gate 3 — Does the design carry it, on-brand, not slop?

_Lens: Design._

- **Decide:** Does this design carry the one claim and read as yours, or is it
  polished, generic filler? Ship, adjust, or send back.
- **Good judgment:** Judge against the brand and the claim, not "does it look nice."
  Catch the generic output that could belong to any startup. On-brand isn't "looks
  nice," it's "could only be us."
- **Not your lens:** This is where the taste profile earns its keep — the agent has a
  standard to check against instead of guessing. Ask it to critique its own output
  against your taste profile and name what's generic. One rule you can judge: swap the
  logo for a competitor's. If it still fits, it's not yet yours.
- **Failure modes:** Shipping on-trend slop because it's technically clean. Judging by
  "looks nice." Letting the generator's default aesthetic become your brand by
  accident.

### Gate 4 — Is the copy true, in-voice, nothing fabricated?

_Lens: Content._

- **Decide:** Is every claim something you can stand behind, in words that sound like
  you? Approve, edit, or send back. This is where fabrication has to die.
- **Good judgment:** Check every specific against reality — a number, a testimonial, a
  capability claim. Run it through your own voice so it doesn't read like a template.
  Rather ship a plainer true sentence than a punchier one you can't defend.
- **Not your lens:** The voice profile is the standard here. Have the agent draft,
  then rewrite in your voice, then read every factual claim and ask "can I prove
  this?" If a stat or quote appeared that you didn't supply, assume it was invented
  and cut it until you can source it.
- **Failure modes:** Shipping a fabricated statistic because it sounded plausible.
  Publishing in generic marketing voice instead of yours. Trusting persuasive copy
  without checking a single claim.

### Gate 5 — Right channel, format, time?

_Lens: Marketing / Content._

- **Decide:** Match the channel and format to where the audience actually is, and pick
  a time you can be present for what follows. Distribution, not creation.
- **Good judgment:** Put the piece where the audience already gathers, in the format
  that channel rewards, not the format that was easy to make. Don't publish something
  that sparks conversation right before you step away from it.
- **Not your lens:** Ask the agent where your specific audience spends time and what
  format performs there, then reshape rather than cross-posting one shape everywhere.
  Timing rule: don't publish what you can't be around to respond to. A free-tier
  scheduler helps you place it. The where-and-when is your call.
- **Failure modes:** Blasting the same asset to every channel. Wrong format for the
  channel. Publishing into a window you can't watch, so the conversation dies
  untended.

## Feedback cadence — continuous, closes the loop

### Gate 6-measure — What happened, and did it feed the next build?

_Lens: Positioning and Marketing._

- **Decide:** Read the real outcome against the one you named at Gate 6-define, and
  turn what you learn into a signal for the build track. Did it move the metric that
  mattered, and what should exist next because of what you saw?
- **Good judgment:** Measure against the outcome you defined, not the number that
  looks best. Treat a real customer signal as input to the build track's first gate
  ("should the next thing exist"), not a vanity trophy. Let outcome data, not opinion,
  decide what gets built next.
- **Not your lens:** Ignore the dashboard's headline number and go back to the
  Gate 6-define outcome — did that specific thing happen? Have the agent pull just
  that and summarize what the response says customers actually want. Write the summary
  where the build side will read it, so the loop closes.
- **Failure modes:** Celebrating vanity metrics. Measuring against a target you
  redefined to look good. Learning something real from the market and never feeding it
  back into what you build.

---

## Using this reference

Run Gate ∞ to size the piece. If you're setting strategy, work G1 and G6-define. For
each artifact, run the per-piece gates in order: ∞ → G0 → G2 → G3 → G4 → G5. On a
rhythm, run G6-measure and feed what you learn back to the build side.

**The loop closes at G6-measure.** Its output — real customer signals — is the input
to the build track's Gate 0 (should the next thing exist). The two kits talk through
the Foundation vault: G6 writes signals there, and the build side reads them. The
gates are the asset. The tools that fill the space between them are a snapshot — see
`reference/tools.md`.
